Conflict of Interest (CoI)

Exam Answer Definition: A Conflict of Interest occurs when an individual’s personal interests (financial, family, friendships) clash with their professional responsibilities, creating a risk of biased decision-making.

Types of CoI (Detailed)

⚡ Actual CoI
Present, real conflict. Example: A procurement officer awards a contract to his own wife’s company despite higher bids from others.
⚠️ Potential CoI
No conflict now, but circumstances may create one. Example: An employee starts a side business that could later compete with employer.
👁️ Apparent CoI
Objectively looks like a conflict even if the person acts fairly. Example: A manager promotes a close friend; others believe favoritism even if friend was best candidate.

CoI vs Moonlighting

Moonlighting = holding a second job after regular hours. It is generally ethical unless it harms the primary job. Moonlighting becomes a CoI when: (1) second job is with a direct competitor, (2) it causes tiredness affecting primary duties, or (3) confidential information is misused.

Example: A senior accountant works for Bank A from 9–5 and secretly does bookkeeping for Bank B at night. This is an actual conflict because he may share sensitive strategies.

Long Answer Tip: Always mention that organizations should require disclosure of outside employment to manage CoI.

Whistle Blowing

Full English Answer Definition: Whistle blowing is the voluntary reporting of illegal, immoral, or illegitimate practices within an organization to internal or external authorities. The whistleblower acts as an ethical alarm.

Internal vs External Whistle Blowing

  • Internal: Reporting to supervisor, HR, or compliance dept. Safer for the employee. Example: An employee reports expense fraud to the ethics hotline.
  • External: Reporting to media, regulators, police, or NGOs. Used when internal channels are corrupt or blocked. Example: A factory worker reveals illegal dumping to the Environmental Protection Agency.

Edward Snowden Case (Real-life external whistleblowing)

Who: Edward Snowden, NSA contractor (USA).
Action: In 2013, he leaked classified documents exposing mass surveillance of ordinary citizens’ emails, calls, and internet data without warrants.
Outcome: Public debate: hero (protecting privacy) vs traitor (violating secrecy laws). He now lives in exile.
Exam relevance: Shows the extreme risks of external whistleblowing. It highlights the need for strong internal protection mechanisms.

Conclusion for exam: Whistleblowing is ethically justifiable when serious harm to public interest exists, but legal consequences may be severe.

Intellectual Property (IP) & Copyright Act of Bangladesh

Notion of IP: Creations of the mind — inventions, literary works, art, symbols. Protected by patents, copyrights, trademarks. IP gives exclusive rights to creators.

Features of a Patent

  • Exclusive Right: Only the patentee can make/use/sell the invention.
  • Limited Term: Usually 20 years from filing date.
  • Public Disclosure: Full description published to enrich public knowledge.

Bangladesh Copyright Act 2023 (Key Provisions)

  • Protects expression, not ideas: You cannot copy the exact text of a book, but you may write on the same theme.
  • Duration: Lifetime of the author + 60 years after death (for literary, dramatic, musical, artistic works).
  • Anti-piracy: Unauthorized reproduction, distribution, or digital sharing is punishable by fine up to BDT 500,000 and/or imprisonment up to 4 years.
  • Automatic protection: No registration needed — copyright exists from creation. Registration provides legal evidence.
Example: Hasan Ahmed Sojib (Copyright Owner)
Hasan Ahmed Sojib, a Bangladeshi poet and novelist, writes a bestselling novel "Moner Janala" in 2025. Under the Copyright Act 2023:
  • He has exclusive right to print, publish, and sell the book.
  • If a publishing house copies it without permission, that is infringement.
  • He can sue for damages and seek criminal prosecution.
  • After his death, his heirs will own the copyright for 60 more years.
Exam note: Always mention that digital piracy (uploading PDF without consent) is also a violation under Section 80 of the Act.

In 2024, Bangladesh amended the act to strengthen enforcement against online copyright infringement.

Normative Ethical Theories

Utilitarianism (Greatest Good Principle)

Core: An action is morally right if it produces the greatest net happiness/benefit for the greatest number of people. Focuses on consequences only.

Example: A hospital has only 10 ventilators but 30 critical COVID patients. Utilitarian approach: prioritize patients with highest chance of survival to save the most lives — even if some elderly patients are not treated. Hard but maximizes overall benefit.

Ethical Relativism

Core: Moral principles are not universal; right/wrong depends on culture, society, or individual perspective. No absolute ethical standards.

Example: In some cultures, giving cash gifts to public officials to expedite paperwork is considered a cultural norm ("baksheesh"). In Western countries, the same act is criminal bribery. Ethical relativism says both can be “right” within their own context — but this theory is criticized for potentially justifying harmful practices.

Examiner's expectation: Contrast both theories. Utilitarianism provides a universal calculation; relativism respects diversity but lacks moral progress.

Computer Crimes & Prevention

Four Main Motives

Financial Gain
Hacking bank accounts, ransomware, credit card theft.
Revenge
Disgruntled employee deleting critical data.
Thrill/Ego
Hackers breaking into systems just for challenge or reputation.
Espionage
Stealing trade secrets or government intelligence.

Five Prevention Strategies (Exam-ready)

  • 🔐 Strong Passwords + 2FA: Use 12+ characters, enable two-factor authentication for email and sensitive accounts.
  • 🛡️ Antivirus & Firewall: Keep updated security software to block malware and unauthorized access.
  • 💾 Regular Data Backup: Offsite or cloud backup. In case of ransomware, restore without paying.
  • 🚪 Access Control (Least Privilege): Employees only see files necessary for their role. Top secrets restricted.
  • 📚 Employee Training: 90% of breaches start with phishing. Teach staff to check email headers, avoid suspicious links.
Real Case: In 2021, a garment factory in Dhaka lost BDT 2 crore due to a phishing attack. After implementing 2FA and security training, no further incidents occurred.

Essay conclusion: Prevention is always cheaper than recovery — organizations must adopt multi-layered cyber hygiene.
